Have you ever wondered what makes a customer buy a specific product? And later on, what makes them become loyal or abandon the company forever? In the times of rapid growth of e-commerce it seems unbelievably easy to gather huge amounts of feedback in form of online customer reviews. Except for being a valuable source of information for other potential buyers they can become an important component of development for the business. How? Shortly, it’s all about text mining.

“If you want to understand people, especially your customers… then you have to be able to possess a strong capability to analyze text” — Paul Hoffmann

For my first text mining project with Python I decided to use a database from Amazon (source: kaggle.com) with over ten thousand customer reviews. The comments are concerning Bluetooth earphones bought via the platform. It’s a great example of real unstructured textual data and thus it’s a perfect set for applying text mining tools and discovering hidden information about the consumers’ preferences and feelings.

The code, helped me answer a couple of crucial questions:

What do customers value in a particular type of product?

What are the functionalities that disappointed them mostly?

Are the customers expressing more emotional or logical approach in the product evaluation?

Sentiment Analysis

Each review in the database has a corresponding rating 1–5 which helps to quickly understand whether a customer would recommend a product or not. It means, we can say if the opinion is positive or negative just by looking at its rating. However, there is a Python tool for sentiment analysis provided by TextBlob which can actually estimate the same using only the content of the comment itself. The question that automatically appears in my head is: Is the review rating received by applying sentiment analysis going to be the same as the one actually given by buyers? After executing the code and analyzing the results I notice the following discrepancies.

There are some opinions with very high rating given by user (5) but estimated as negative by TextBlob. Analogically, there are reviews with very low rating given by user (1) with a positive sentiment according to the tool used. To get a better understanding I look into some of them.

I assume those are simply human errors thus I decide to exclude them from my database to get more accurate results while applying text classification models later on.

Text pre-processing

At first, I assume that ratings 1–2 present negative feelings regarding the product while ratings 4–5 a positive one. I exclude from the database reviews with the rating 3 given by user as they present neither a positive nor a negative sentiment.

As a first step in cleaning the data and preparing for the analysis I change all text to lower case. It is important in order the machine not to treat two or more identical for human words separately just because they were written differently (e.g. “Earphone” and “earphone”). The next move is to remove punctuation, emojis and digits as I want to keep alphabetic characters only.

One additional but crucial step that will help generating better results is using a list of stopwords, which simply means that I remove all the words from my database that don’t bring any meaningful value. Furthermore, I create my own list of context specific words to better fit the particular case I’m working on.

In the last step I use PorterStemmer in order to convert the words to their basic form and again, minimize the risk of getting the machine count words with identical meaning multiple times. The word minimize is crucial here as the stemming tool has its limitations.

At this point my data is ready to finally discover the customers feelings.

Exploratory analysis

Going back to the questions asked at the beginning of this article:

What do customers value in a particular type of product?

Looking into the graph above it is visible that among the most frequently mentioned functionalities were those regarding the product quality (“qualiti”), the bass (“bass”) and the battery (“batteri”). It allows me to believe that while buying this type of product, consumers primarily evaluate the level of product quality and pay greatest attention to the bass generated by the device and the battery life. As “price” is also at the top of the list it indicates that cost is probably an aspect of great importance while choosing bluetooth headphones.

The most common words appearing in reviews of satisfied customers only seem not to differ significantly from those presented in general opinions. What is new are the frequent terms indicating positive emotions of buyers such as “amaz”, “love” or “excel”.

What are the functionalities that disappointed customers mostly?

In the case of unsatisfied consumers reviews there are numerous words with a clear negative tone, e.g. “Stop”, “dont”, “bad”, “worst” or “poor”. One of the most common issues presented in this review set are connectivity and charging problems. The presence of the word “call” on the list referring to unsatisfied consumers and the fact of its non-existence in a positive reviews list can potentially indicate that the function of voice calls more often disappoints customers than it is enjoyed by them. The words “replac” and “return” may indicate that users are considering exchange, repair or return of items. Further conclusions can be drawn knowing more about the product specification.

Another convenient way of presenting the above information are charts in the form of word clouds. They can fit several hundred words or collocations in a relatively small field playing with the font size.

Subjectivity

In the next step, using the previously mentioned TextBlob library, I discover the subjectivity of opinions written by Amazon users.

Are the customers expressing more emotional or logical approach in the product evaluation?

The tool I use is sentiment analysis and especially its property defined as “Subjectivity”. By default the results are presented in the range of 0–1, where 0 is an objective opinion and 1 is a subjective statement. I decide to further divide the comments into three subsets. The first is a set of objective opinions including those with the result of 0–0.333 and the second is a set of subjective opinions (0.666–1). To be more accurate I also separated a neutral set, which includes statements of difficult to determine degree of subjectivity (the score “in the middle” 0.333–0.666).

Among the positive set most of the comments are ambiguous. There are almost twice as many objective than subjective reviews. On average, every fifth positive opinion is a logical and meaningful message that can be described as an objective view.

Analyzing the negative reviews, over a third of them are objective, and again, only one in eight statements present a subjective view. According to the results, negative reviews were more often objective than the positive ones as the latter were in majority difficult to categorize.

To sum up

Identifying the most popular expressions at the exploratory analysis stage made it possible to obtain the most frequently raised issues by satisfied as well as unsatisfied users. Particularly noteworthy are the problems mentioned by unsatisfied consumers as they can provide a valuable source of knowledge for product developers and thus make the defects of the product eliminated or at least minimized by improving failing functionalities. Similarly, extracting positive emotions expressed by consumers can help to get better knowledge regarding the buyers and get more familiar with “their language” which can be further used for marketing purposes.

The examination of the subjectivity level of consumer opinions and its division into three subsets enables to present the shares of subjective, objective and difficult to determine reviews in the entire set. This method helps to answer the questions to what extent negative or positive opinions carry a logical message or how emotional they are. The above knowledge allows the better understanding of consumers and their approach to a given product, for example, whether customers value more the technical attributes of the purchased product or the experience related to its use.
